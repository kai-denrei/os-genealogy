How to Read

Each horizontal bar runs from an operating system’s release year to its end of active development (or to today, if still living). Bars are stacked within each house to avoid overlap; the vertical order within a house carries no meaning.

Lineage Lines

Solid curves mark direct code or kernel descent. Dashed curves mark conceptual or clean-room inheritance — Linux from Minix, ReactOS from NT, FreeDOS from MS-DOS. Multiple parent lines converging on one child indicate a merger.

What’s Missing

Hundreds of operating systems. Hobby kernels, defunct experiments, regional variants, and most embedded RTOSes are absent on purpose. This is a constellation, not an atlas. For the atlas, see Eylenburg’s 1,300-entry family tree.

Editorial Bias

Notes lean dry-witty over neutral. Dates default to year of first general release; a few are contested. Where lineage is genuinely ambiguous (Linux’s debt to Minix, NT’s to VMS) the dashed line is the editorial compromise.

Drawn from public history at Wikipedia, Eylenburg’s OS Family Tree, Salus’s A Quarter Century of Unix, and Showstopper! by Zachary. Errors and contested datings remain the editor’s.
